# Cookbook

Four full walkthroughs of Sunday's core workflows, using made-up content. The point is to show you what good runs *look* like before you do your own — what the assistant says, what files end up where, what the log entry reads like.

| # | File | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| 01 | `01-first-triage.md` | Your first triage. Three inbox items → three wiki pages, with the assistant asking the right clarifying questions. |
| 02 | `02-ingesting-a-video.md` | Ingesting a YouTube talk end-to-end: download, summary, source page, topic updates, log entry. |
| 03 | `03-asking-the-vault.md` | A query that pulls from three pages, answers with citations, and saves the synthesis as a new wiki page. |
| 04 | `04-running-a-lint.md` | A lint pass that catches orphans, contradictions, and stale claims, and produces an actionable report. |

These are aspirational examples. Your first runs won't look this clean — they'll be slower, you'll be more uncertain about which area something belongs in, the assistant will ask more questions. That's normal. Within a week or two, the routine settles.

## How to use these examples

- **Read 01 before your first triage.** It is the most opinion-setting workflow — get this right and the rest fall into place.
- **Read 02 the first time you ingest a video.** The "5–15 pages touched" rule is real and the example shows what that means concretely.
- **Read 03 before saving a query result.** Quick reference for citation style and the "save as new page" decision.
- **Read 04 the first time you say "lint".** What you should expect in the report, what each section is for.

You don't need to read these in order. Skim now, refer back when you actually need them.

## A note on the made-up content

The examples use a fictional researcher named Maya Liu who is building a Sunday vault about her PhD work on **typography legibility for low-vision readers**. Her vault has clients (universities, accessibility consultancies), projects (a thesis, a workshop she's giving), topics (typography, vision science), and a feed of academic papers and conference talks. Maya isn't a real person — but her vault feels real enough to demonstrate everything.

If your use-case is very different (you're a developer, an entrepreneur, a homeschooler), the **shape** of the workflows is the same. Substitute your nouns.
